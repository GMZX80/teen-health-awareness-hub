Practical parent and carer FAQ

School Anxiety, SEND and Services FAQ

Plain-English answers to the questions families often ask when school, health, local authority, social care and work pressures all collide. England-wide duties are described with Northumberland as the local example.

Boundary

Use this to prepare conversations, not as legal or medical advice

Rules, policies and thresholds can change, and individual facts matter. Use the live sources, ask professionals to put decisions in writing, and seek urgent help if a young person cannot stay safe, has taken an overdose, has serious injury, is missing, or there is immediate danger.

First practical move

Ask for written decisions, owners and review dates

The strongest family position is usually calm, written and specific. Ask what duty or policy is being relied on, what evidence has been considered, who owns the next action, and when the plan will be reviewed.

Ask school for

  • Attendance support plan and review date.
  • SEN support or reasonable adjustment plan.
  • Named trusted adult and contact route.
  • Written details of any reduced timetable.

Ask the local authority for

  • Whether section 19 education has been considered.
  • Whether EHC needs assessment is appropriate.
  • Transport decision and appeal route if provision changes.
  • Which team owns the case and response timescale.

Ask services for

  • What consent is being requested and why.
  • What information will be shared and with whom.
  • What should happen if risk increases.
  • What support exists while waiting.

School

Adjustments, attendance and curriculum

School anxiety should be treated as distress and barriers to understand, not as a simple refusal. Schools still need a lawful, reviewable education plan.

Can school make any adjustment they want for anxiety?

No. Schools can and should make reasonable individual adjustments where anxiety, SEND or disability affects attendance or access to learning. But the adjustment should be lawful, proportionate, recorded, reviewed and connected to the young person's needs.

A good adjustment lowers a barrier while keeping education in view: quiet arrival, trusted adult, exit card, reduced social exposure, adapted timetable, modified PE arrangements, safer changing arrangements, homework adjustment or staged return.

What kinds of school adjustments can parents ask about?

Useful adjustments are usually specific to a barrier. Parents can ask about arrival and leaving arrangements, a named adult, reduced transitions, a quiet place, toilet or medical pass, modified PE or changing arrangements, seating, homework load, sensory breaks, lunch/break support, flexible uniform, reduced public questioning, lesson previews, movement breaks, phased return and a clear plan for panic or shutdown.

Ask school to write the adjustment as: barrier, adjustment, owner, how the child uses it, how adults will respond, how success will be measured and review date.

Do adjustments have to be permanent?

No. Some adjustments are short-term scaffolding; others may be needed long term. The key is that removal should be planned and evidence-led, not sudden. If an adjustment is helping the child attend or learn, that is evidence that it may still be needed.

A good review asks: what has improved, what still depends on the adjustment, what would happen if it was reduced, and how will school spot early signs of relapse?

How should school test whether an adjustment can be reduced?

Use a staged trial. Change one thing at a time, agree what data will be watched, keep the old support available as a fallback, and set a short review date. For example, a child might keep the trusted adult and quiet arrival while slowly increasing lesson time, rather than losing several supports at once.

The question is not "can we remove support because attendance looks better?" It is "which support is still carrying the improvement?"

Can school remove PE completely if PE is a trigger?

Sometimes PE may need to be adapted, reduced or temporarily changed, especially if distress, health, disability or safeguarding concerns are involved. But removing PE indefinitely is harder to justify because PE is part of the national curriculum for maintained schools at key stages 1 to 4.

Ask for the barrier to be named. Is the problem changing rooms, kit, social exposure, body image, sensory overwhelm, physical symptoms, a particular activity, bullying or fear of panic? The answer should shape the adjustment.

Can school use a reduced or part-time timetable?

Only carefully. DfE attendance guidance says pupils are entitled to full-time education. A part-time timetable should be exceptional, time-limited, recorded, reviewed and used to support access to education rather than quietly manage a difficult situation.

Ask for the timetable in writing with the reason, end or review date, education being missed, how work will be covered, and the steps back toward fuller attendance.

Can anxiety-related absence be authorised?

It can be. DfE guidance recognises that mental-health issues can affect attendance. Schools should work with families to understand barriers, make support plans and involve other services where needed.

If school says absence cannot be authorised, ask what evidence would change that view and whether medical, SEND or local authority duties have been considered.

Does a child need a diagnosis before school helps?

No. A diagnosis can help explain need, but support should be based on observed barriers and impact. SEND support and reasonable adjustments can start from need, not just from a formal label.

What if a child improves, support is removed, and then they relapse?

Treat this as evidence, not failure. If the child was attending while adjustments were in place and relapsed when they were removed, the adjustment may have been part of what made education accessible. Ask school to reinstate the effective support quickly, review what changed, and step down more slowly if appropriate.

Parents can ask for a "relapse plan" before support is reduced: early warning signs, who the child tells, what gets reinstated automatically, when parents are contacted, and when the plan returns to SENCO or attendance review.

Can school say support is no longer needed because attendance improved?

Improved attendance is a good sign, but it does not automatically prove the barrier has gone. It may show that the support is working. A safer approach is to review attendance, distress, learning, recovery time at home, incidents, health symptoms and the young person's view before deciding what changes.

What if an adjustment helps attendance but means missing some learning?

That can happen during recovery, but it needs a plan. Ask how missed learning will be covered, how the child will stay connected to the curriculum, and when the arrangement will be reviewed. A support plan should not quietly become long-term loss of education.

What should a useful school meeting produce?

It should produce actions, not just sympathy. Ask for: the young person's views, school observations, current attendance pattern, known triggers, adjustments tried, what worked, what did not, next attendance step, responsible person, review date and escalation route.

Local authority

Education, SEND, transport and alternative provision

The local authority becomes especially important when a child is not receiving suitable education, may need statutory SEND assessment, or is being offered education somewhere other than their usual school.

If a child cannot attend school because of health needs, who arranges education?

Local authorities have duties to arrange suitable education for children of compulsory school age who would otherwise not receive it because of illness, exclusion or other reasons. DfE statutory guidance covers children who cannot attend school or can attend only intermittently because of physical or mental health needs.

Northumberland has a local policy on arranging education for children who cannot attend school because of health needs. Ask whether the case has been considered under that policy and under section 19 duties.

If temporary provision or another school is suggested, who provides transport?

It depends on who has arranged the provision, distance, safety, disability, SEND, age and the local transport policy. If the local authority says another placement or alternative provision is suitable, ask at the same time how the child is expected to get there and who will pay.

In Northumberland, SEND transport is assessed by the council, and the SEND Local Offer says transport can be considered as a reasonable adjustment where disability means it is needed, even inside the usual walking distance.

What if the suggested school is miles away and too far to walk?

Ask whether it is the nearest suitable school and whether the child is an eligible child for free home-to-school travel. DfE statutory guidance says a child under 8 is eligible for free travel to their nearest suitable school if it is more than 2 miles from home; a child aged 8 or over is eligible if it is more than 3 miles away.

Distance is not the only question. A child may also be eligible where there is no safe walking route, or where they cannot reasonably be expected to walk because of SEND, disability or mobility problems. For an anxiety/SEND case, ask for the travel decision to consider distress, safety, supervision, ability to use public transport, journey length, transitions and whether travel itself would block access to education.

Can the council say parents must provide transport to a temporary school?

They should not simply assume that. If the local authority or school is proposing a different site because the usual school is not currently accessible, ask for a written transport decision. The decision should explain whether the placement is the nearest suitable provision, whether statutory walking distance applies, whether SEND/disability/mobility or route safety applies, and how the child is realistically expected to attend.

What should parents put in a transport request?

Include the proposed school or provision, distance from home, age, route concerns, public transport practicality, SEND/disability/health needs, anxiety triggers, supervision needs, risk during travel, start and finish times, parent work constraints, and why the journey would prevent attendance without support.

Ask for the decision, reasons, policy relied on, review or appeal route, and what interim travel support will be provided while the decision is considered.

Can parents request an EHC needs assessment?

Yes. Parents, young people and schools can ask the local authority for an EHC needs assessment. This may be relevant where ordinary SEN support is not enough, attendance plans repeatedly fail, or anxiety appears linked to learning, communication, sensory, social or developmental needs.

If the local authority refuses to assess, refuses to issue a plan, or issues a plan the family disagrees with, there are appeal routes to the SEND Tribunal.

Can the local authority fine parents when anxiety is involved?

Attendance enforcement exists, but DfE guidance expects support-first work and careful consideration of individual circumstances. Where mental health, SEND, disability, medical evidence or lack of suitable education is relevant, families should ask for those factors to be recorded before enforcement is considered.

Ask: "What support has been offered, what barriers have been identified, and how have mental health, SEND and section 19 duties been considered before enforcement?"

Health services

Clinical advice, evidence and waiting times

Health professionals can advise on symptoms, mental-health needs and risk. Schools and local authorities still need to translate advice into education support. Waiting for specialist help should not mean pausing school support, safety planning or local early help.

Does anxiety count as a health need?

It can. Mental-health needs can be relevant to attendance, education planning and section 19 consideration. Evidence might come from a GP, CYPS/CAMHS, paediatrician, psychologist, school nurse, therapist or another relevant professional.

What clinical evidence is most useful for school or the local authority?

The most useful evidence describes functioning, not just diagnosis. Ask clinicians to set out what the young person can and cannot currently manage, what symptoms show up in school or travel, whether attendance is affected by anxiety or physical symptoms, what risks matter, what adjustments are recommended, and when the advice should be reviewed.

Useful wording is practical: "cannot currently manage busy unstructured lunch without support", "needs a graded return", "panic symptoms are triggered by crowded transitions", "requires access to a quiet space", or "symptoms have prevented reliable attendance".

Can health professionals tell school exactly what to do?

They can make recommendations, but the school and local authority usually decide how education is delivered. The most useful clinical letters are practical: what the young person cannot currently manage, what risks or symptoms matter, what adjustments are recommended, and when the advice should be reviewed.

What should parents tell a GP or referrer?

NHS advice says it helps to give as much information as possible about how the young person has been feeling, how long it has been going on, and how it affects day-to-day life. For school anxiety, include attendance pattern, sleep, eating, nausea, panic, self-harm concerns, bullying or trauma worries, family impact, what school has tried, and what helps even slightly.

Bring a one-page summary if possible: timeline, current risks, current school plan, services involved, medications, physical symptoms and what you are asking for.

What makes a CYPS/CAMHS referral stronger?

A stronger referral usually includes consent, the young person's views, parent/carer concerns, school evidence, attendance data, functioning at home and school, risk information, physical-health context, previous support tried, what changed, and why specialist help is needed now.

Northumberland CYPS asks referrers to consider criteria including Northumberland GP registration, age range, informed consent, assessment of need for professional referrals, and previous interventions. The referral form also asks about school/college, GP details, consent to contact education, other agencies and whether information may be passed to another more appropriate service.

What if the child is waiting for CYPS/CAMHS?

Waiting should not mean nothing happens. NHS advice says people should be told how long it may take to get help, and should let the service know if things change or get worse. Ask for the expected wait for first contact, expected wait for treatment, who to contact while waiting, and what information should trigger re-triage.

Ask school and local services what can be done now: attendance support, SEN support, school nurse input, Family Hub/Early Help, Primary Mental Health Worker or Mental Health Support Team if available, GP review and risk plan.

What should school do while a child waits for specialist input?

School should continue to support access to education. DfE mental-health guidance for schools emphasises identifying need and monitoring the impact of interventions. That means school can keep adjusting the day, reviewing attendance barriers, involving SENCO/pastoral staff, gathering evidence and escalating if risk or impairment increases.

A useful question is: "What are we doing for the next two weeks, not just what referral are we waiting for?"

What if the referral is rejected or redirected?

Ask for the reason in writing and the recommended next route. A rejection does not mean the child has no need; it may mean the service thinks another route is more appropriate, the threshold is not met, consent or information is missing, or lower-level support should be tried first.

Ask: "What evidence would change the decision?", "Which service should hold the plan now?", "Who is responsible for risk while we follow the suggested route?", and "Can the referrer speak to the Single Point of Access or consultation route?"

When should parents update the service while waiting?

Update the service if attendance collapses, self-harm or suicidal thoughts appear or worsen, eating or drinking becomes unsafe, panic escalates, the child cannot leave the house, aggression or running away starts, family supervision becomes unsustainable, medication changes, or school says the placement is at risk.

Put the update in writing where possible: what changed, when, how often, current risk, what support is in place, and what response you are asking for.

When is waiting no longer safe?

Do not wait for a routine appointment if there is immediate danger, overdose, serious injury, the young person cannot stay safe, there is a suicidal plan or intent, severe self-harm, severe restriction of food or fluid, psychosis-like symptoms, exploitation or abuse concern, or the family cannot safely supervise.

Use urgent routes such as NHS 111 mental-health option, 999, A&E, crisis services or safeguarding routes depending on the risk and urgency.

Can parents ask for advice before a full referral?

Yes, in many local systems. Northumberland CYPS lists a Single Point of Access consultation slot route, and Be You Northumberland describes school nursing, Early Help, Primary Mental Health Worker and graduated mental-health support routes. Parents can ask school, GP or local support whether a consultation route is available before or alongside referral.

Should parents wait for a diagnosis before asking for education support?

No. Diagnosis may help, but education support should be based on current barriers and impact. A child can need attendance support, SEN support, reasonable adjustments or health-needs consideration while clinical assessment is still pending.

What should parents keep as evidence during the wait?

Keep a dated log of attendance, panic or shutdown episodes, sleep, eating, nausea or pain, self-harm or risk comments, school contacts, adjustments tried, what helped, what made things worse, appointments, referrals, rejected referrals and changes at home. This helps clinicians, school and the local authority see the pattern rather than isolated incidents.

What can parents ask at the first appointment?

Ask what the clinician thinks is happening, what risk level they see, what treatment or support is recommended, what school should do now, whether a written summary will be sent, who receives it, what to do if things worsen, and when the next review will be.

If school attendance is affected, ask for recommendations in functional language that school can act on.

Home and work

When the plan lands on the family

Families often end up carrying the gap between services. That does not mean statutory education, health, SEND or care duties disappear.

What does it mean when "the plan lands on the family"?

It means the family is expected to cover the practical gap: staying home, supervising distress, managing risk, transporting to different provision, chasing services, collecting evidence, and keeping work or siblings going. Families often do this because they love the child, but that does not make it a sustainable or lawful replacement for education, health, SEND or social-care planning.

Ask professionals to separate what the family can reasonably do from what a service is responsible for assessing, arranging or funding.

Can services assume a parent will be available all day?

They should not simply assume that. Parents have responsibilities, but they may also have employment, health needs, other children, caring responsibilities, financial limits and safety limits. If a plan depends on a parent being home, driving long distances, supervising risk or delivering education, ask for that assumption to be written down and assessed.

A useful question is: "What happens if I cannot safely or practically provide this level of care during working hours?"

Can an organisation advise a parent to give up work?

They can discuss practical pressures, but "give up your job" should not be presented as the plan instead of assessing the child and family's needs. If anyone says this, ask them to put it in writing with the legal basis and the alternatives considered.

A useful response is: "Please assess what support my child needs to access education and what support I need as a parent carer. I cannot be expected to solve an education or care gap by leaving employment."

What if the family is told to keep the child at home because school cannot manage?

Ask whether this is authorised absence, illness absence, a reduced timetable, exclusion, suspension, off-site direction, alternative provision, section 19 education, or simply an informal arrangement. Informal "keep them home for now" arrangements can leave families carrying the problem without a clear duty-holder.

Ask for the education plan in writing: who is providing education, how many hours, what curriculum, what contact with school, what review date, and what support is being put in place to restore access.

What if a parent has to stay home because the child cannot attend school?

Employment rights exist, but they are limited. Time off for dependants can cover emergencies and is usually unpaid. Longer-term arrangements may involve unpaid parental leave, carer's leave, flexible working, annual leave or employer discretion.

Ask the employer about practical options, but also keep pushing the education and support plan. A parent's work problem may be evidence that the current support arrangement is not sustainable.

What employment rights might be relevant?

Time off for dependants is for unexpected problems or emergencies and is usually unpaid. Carer's leave can provide unpaid leave for caring responsibilities. Parental leave may be available for children up to 18. Flexible working can be requested, including changes to hours, times or place of work.

These rights can help manage short-term reality, but they do not transfer education, SEND, health or social-care duties onto the parent.

Can a parent ask for their own needs to be assessed?

Yes, where a child has disabilities or additional needs, parent carers may be able to ask for their own needs to be assessed. Northumberland's short breaks guidance says parent carers have a right to an assessment of their own under the Children and Families Act 2014, on the appearance of need or where the parent requests one.

Ask for a parent carer needs assessment if caring is affecting work, sleep, health, finances, relationships, siblings, safety or the ability to keep providing care.

Can parents ask social care for help without being accused of failing?

Yes. Families can ask for support. Depending on the facts, this might involve Early Help, a child-in-need assessment, disability support, short breaks, a parent carer needs assessment or safeguarding assessment.

What support can children's social care or disabled children's services consider?

Depending on eligibility and assessment, support might include Early Help, a Child and Family Assessment, short breaks, day activities, direct payments, home support, equipment or adaptations, support for siblings, or a plan around family stress and safety.

In Northumberland, the Disabled Children's Team and short breaks pages say families can request a Child and Family Assessment through the First Contact Team, and that short breaks are considered where a social-care assessment identifies that support is needed.

What are short breaks?

Short breaks are support that can give disabled children experiences outside the usual family caring routine and give families a break from caring responsibilities. They can look different depending on the child's needs and local eligibility, and they usually follow assessment.

In Northumberland, families who think their child may benefit from short breaks can contact the First Contact Team. A Child and Family Assessment may be needed where needs cannot be met through universal or targeted services.

What if siblings or family life are being affected?

Say so clearly. Parent carer assessment and family assessment should consider the wider family, not only the child who is struggling with school. Record impacts on siblings, sleep, work, finances, parental health, family relationships and whether other children are taking on caring roles.

What evidence should parents keep when family life is absorbing the gap?

Keep dated notes of work missed, hours spent supervising, travel demands, incidents, risk concerns, impact on siblings, costs, sleep disruption, health effects, emails chasing services, and what support was requested. This evidence helps show that the current arrangement is not just inconvenient, but unsustainable.

How can parents push back without sounding uncooperative?

Use calm, practical wording: "We are willing to work with the plan, but this level of responsibility is not sustainable without assessment and support." Ask for the duty, decision, owner and review date. If the plan relies on the family, ask what support the family will receive to make it realistic.

Consent and information sharing

What can happen with or without consent?

Consent is not one single rule. It depends on the action, the young person's age and understanding, safeguarding risk, data protection rules, clinical treatment and who has parental responsibility.

Can school or services share information without parent consent?

Sometimes, yes. Schools and agencies can record concerns, discuss them internally and share proportionate information where there is a lawful basis. For safeguarding, consent is not always required, and data protection should not be used as a reason to avoid necessary safeguarding action.

What usually needs consent?

Routine referrals to external support usually involve consent from a parent, carer or competent young person. Northumberland IASS says settings may seek advice from education, health or social care professionals and will seek consent from parents or young people for this.

Who consents to medical treatment?

Usually someone with parental responsibility consents for a child, unless the young person has enough understanding to consent themselves. NHS guidance describes this as Gillick competence for under-16s. A young person's confidentiality can also matter, particularly as they get older.

What should parents ask before agreeing to a referral?

Ask what is being referred, why, what information will be shared, who will receive it, whether the young person will be seen, what waiting times are likely, what happens if the referral is rejected, and what support is available while waiting.

When stuck

Escalation questions that keep the issue precise

Use these questions when meetings keep circling without a decision.

School

  • "What support has been tried, for how long, and what was the impact?"
  • "Which part of the school day is currently unmanageable?"
  • "Is this being considered as SEND, disability, health need, attendance, safeguarding, or more than one?"

Local authority

  • "Is my child currently receiving suitable full-time education?"
  • "Has section 19 been considered and what is the decision?"
  • "Who is responsible for transport if alternative provision is proposed?"

Health

  • "What symptoms or risk would make this urgent?"
  • "What should school do while waiting for specialist input?"
  • "Can recommendations be written in functional terms?"

Consent

  • "Are you asking for consent, informing me, or making a safeguarding decision?"
  • "What information will be shared, with whom, and why?"
  • "What happens if consent is not given?"
