Safety and urgent help

This site is a curation aid, not clinical advice. If there is immediate risk of harm, a serious injury, overdose, inability to stay safe, or urgent medical symptoms, seek emergency help immediately.

Mental health crisis

Use NHS urgent mental health guidance, local crisis services, NHS 111, 999, or A&E depending on urgency. Do not wait for a routine appointment if safety is at risk.

Self-harm concern

Speak calmly, reduce access to means where possible, and seek professional advice. If there is immediate danger, treat it as an emergency.

Severe vomiting or red flags

Vomiting blood, green/yellow-green vomit in children, stiff neck, severe headache, severe abdominal pain, or signs of serious illness need urgent medical assessment.

Core stance

How to hold the problem

Distress is information

School avoidance, panic, shutdown, anger, nausea, or tears are clues. The job is to understand what the young person is experiencing and what support changes the demand enough to make progress possible.

Compassion plus structure

Validation matters, but so does a predictable plan. Helpful support usually combines emotional safety, small achievable steps, school adjustments, and professional input when needed.

Track patterns, not blame

Record sleep, food, nausea, school demands, social pressure, sensory load, conflict, medication changes, and recovery time. Patterns help conversations with professionals stay concrete.

School avoidance

Emotionally based school avoidance

Many families prefer terms like emotionally based school avoidance or anxiety-related absence because "refusal" can sound like choice or defiance. The goal is to reduce barriers while protecting the young person's dignity.

Questions to investigate

  • Is the distress linked to arrival, lessons, transitions, lunch, crowds, specific people, uncertainty, tests, homework, uniforms, transport, or separation?
  • Is there bullying, friendship rupture, sensory overwhelm, unmet SEND need, trauma, academic pressure, or fear of embarrassment?
  • Does nausea appear mainly before school, during specific lessons, after poor sleep, after meals, or across the whole week?
  • What helps even slightly: later start, safe room, trusted adult, reduced timetable, predictable exit plan, remote catch-up, friend support?

School meeting agenda

  1. Agree shared language: distress/barriers rather than blame.
  2. Ask school what they observe before, during, and after distress spikes.
  3. Request one named adult and one low-friction check-in route.
  4. Agree the smallest attendance step that is genuinely achievable.
  5. Set a review date and avoid changing several variables at once.

Anxiety

Understanding anxiety without feeding it

Signs to notice

Avoidance, reassurance seeking, panic symptoms, irritability, sleep changes, headaches, nausea, stomach pain, tearfulness, perfectionism, or fear of being judged can all fit anxiety patterns.

What tends to help

Warm validation, predictable routines, gradual exposure, problem solving, sleep/food basics, school adjustments, CBT-informed support, and professional care when anxiety disrupts normal life.

What to be careful with

Endless reassurance can briefly calm anxiety while making it louder next time. Sudden pressure can also overwhelm. Aim for small supported steps and review what actually happened.

Nausea and body symptoms

Take symptoms seriously without assuming one cause

Anxiety can show up physically, including nausea, but nausea can also be medical. Use a GP or appropriate clinician to rule out physical causes, especially when symptoms are persistent, worsening, unexplained, or come with red flags.

Track before appointments

  • Time of day, duration, severity, vomiting, pain, bowel changes, dizziness, fever, weight change, hydration, menstrual cycle if relevant.
  • Sleep, meals, caffeine, medication, school demands, social events, panic symptoms, and recovery activities.
  • What helps: food, rest, distraction, leaving a situation, breathing, medication, reassurance, sleep, routine.

Ask a clinician

  • What physical causes should be ruled out?
  • What red flags should trigger urgent care?
  • Could this fit functional nausea, migraine, reflux, constipation, medication side effects, anxiety, or another pattern?
  • What is the plan if symptoms continue for two more weeks?

Working plan

A practical curation loop

1

Capture

Add a source, observation, question, school email summary, or appointment note. Keep personal details minimal.

2

Sort

Tag it as safety, school, anxiety, nausea, sleep, professional support, rights, or lived experience.

3

Decide

Turn it into one next action: ask school, book GP, track symptom, change routine, request review, or park it.

4

Review

Check what changed after a week. Keep what helps; stop what adds pressure without benefit.
