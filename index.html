Safety comes before strategy

This guide is for awareness, preparation and signposting. It is not medical advice, therapy, diagnosis, safeguarding advice, or a substitute for professional care.

If a young person is at immediate risk of harm, has taken an overdose, has a serious injury, cannot stay safe, or has urgent medical symptoms, seek emergency help immediately through local emergency services, A&E, NHS 111/999 as appropriate, or a crisis service.

Mental health crisis

Use NHS urgent mental health guidance, local crisis services, NHS 111, 999, or A&E depending on risk and urgency. Do not wait for a routine appointment if safety is at risk.

Self-harm concern

Working model

A practical way to hold the problem

Teen distress can be emotional, social, educational, physical and neurodevelopmental at the same time. A useful plan avoids blame, gathers patterns, and turns concern into small next actions.

1. Stabilise safety

Know what would make this urgent, who to contact, and what support is available outside normal hours. Make crisis information easy to find.

2. Understand the demand

School avoidance is often a response to demands that feel unmanageable: social pressure, sensory load, academic fear, transitions, uncertainty, separation, bullying or shame.

3. Reduce barriers

Progress usually comes from supported steps: predictable routines, one trusted adult, a safer arrival plan, adjusted expectations, and review dates.

4. Track patterns

Record sleep, food, nausea, panic signs, school events, social stress, sensory triggers, medication changes and recovery time. Patterns make professional conversations concrete.

5. Keep dignity central

Use language like distress, barriers, anxiety-related absence or emotionally based school avoidance. Avoid framing the young person as lazy, manipulative or defiant.

6. Review and adapt

Try one change at a time where possible. Keep what lowers distress or improves engagement. Stop what adds pressure without benefit.

Communication

Helpful parent and carer responses

What to say

  • "I can see this feels really hard. We are going to work out what is making it hard."
  • "You are not in trouble for feeling anxious. We still need a plan."
  • "Let's find the smallest step that feels possible, not perfect."
  • "Your body symptoms matter. We will track them and ask for medical advice if needed."

What to avoid

  • Arguing about whether the feeling is "real".
  • Using threats or shame to force attendance.
  • Assuming nausea is "just anxiety" without considering medical causes.
  • Making every plan in the middle of panic. Plan when everyone is calmer.

Issue library

Quick reads first, deeper detail when needed

Use these sections as a map. Start with the short "what to notice" layer, then open the detail if that issue seems relevant.

Anxiety and panic

Look for avoidance, reassurance loops, panic symptoms, stomach upset, sleep disruption, fear of judgement, perfectionism or shutdown.

Go deeper
  • Separate the trigger from the fear: school gate, classroom, social judgement, tests, separation, uncertainty or sensory overwhelm.
  • Respond with validation plus a small next step: "I believe this feels awful, and we will make a plan."
  • Watch reassurance loops. Reassurance can help briefly, but repeated reassurance may keep anxiety dependent on external checking.
  • Ask for professional help if anxiety blocks school, sleep, eating, friendships, family life or basic daily routines.

Low mood and depression

Notice persistent sadness, loss of interest, irritability, withdrawal, hopelessness, sleep/appetite change, self-critical talk or falling functioning.

Go deeper
  • Teen depression may look like anger, numbness or shutdown rather than obvious sadness.
  • Ask directly and calmly about hopelessness, self-harm and suicidal thoughts if you are worried. Asking does not put the idea there.
  • Keep routines gently alive: food, sleep, daylight, connection, school contact and manageable activity.
  • Seek GP/CAMHS or urgent help depending on severity, risk and speed of change.

Self-harm and suicidal thoughts

Treat any self-harm disclosure, suicidal thought, overdose, injury, goodbye message or inability to stay safe as serious.

Go deeper
  • Stay calm, listen, thank them for telling you, and avoid punishment or shock reactions.
  • Reduce access to immediate means where possible and increase supervision while seeking help.
  • Use urgent services immediately if there is current danger, overdose, serious injury, a plan, or inability to stay safe.
  • After any episode, ask what was happening before it, what made it worse, and what helped them get through the next hour.

School avoidance

See it as distress plus barriers. It may involve anxiety, bullying, SEND needs, sensory load, academic fear, social pressure or body symptoms.

Go deeper
  • Map the school day in detail: leaving home, journey, gate, form time, lessons, lunch, toilets, transitions and homework.
  • Agree one trusted adult, one safe signal, and one realistic next attendance step.
  • Use graded return, reduced demand or adjusted timetable when appropriate, with review dates.
  • Keep education rights/SEND questions separate from attendance blame.

Nausea and physical symptoms

Anxiety can be physical, but nausea, pain, vomiting, dizziness or weight change should not be dismissed.

Go deeper
  • Track timing, food, hydration, sleep, school demands, panic signs, medication, pain, bowel changes and what helps.
  • Ask a GP what physical causes should be ruled out and what red flags should trigger urgent care.
  • Look for patterns: school mornings only, after meals, after poor sleep, around social events, or across the whole week.
  • Do not use "it's anxiety" as a reason to avoid medical review when symptoms persist or worsen.

Sleep

Poor sleep can amplify anxiety, low mood, nausea, concentration difficulty, emotional regulation problems and school avoidance.

Go deeper
  • Start with consistency: wake time, light exposure, wind-down routine and fewer late-night negotiations.
  • For neurodivergent young people, consider sensory comfort, room clutter, transitions, active evenings and screen timing.
  • Avoid unregulated sleep medication. Discuss persistent sleep problems with a clinician.
  • Track sleep alongside anxiety and school demands so patterns become visible.

Eating, body image and weight concerns

Take secretive eating, rapid weight change, fear of weight gain, vomiting, laxatives, excessive exercise or meal avoidance seriously.

Go deeper
  • Eating disorders can be medically serious even when the young person appears "fine".
  • Avoid debates about appearance. Focus on health, mood, energy, concentration and safety.
  • Seek GP advice promptly and ask about local children and young people's eating disorder services if concerns are present.
  • Escalate quickly for fainting, chest pain, severe restriction, dehydration, purging, rapid weight loss or high risk.

OCD and intrusive thoughts

OCD can involve unwanted thoughts, reassurance seeking, checking, washing, counting, mental rituals or avoidance.

Go deeper
  • Intrusive thoughts are not the same as intent. They can be frightening precisely because they clash with the young person's values.
  • Try not to join rituals or repeated reassurance loops, but do not abruptly remove support without a plan.
  • Ask GP/CAMHS about evidence-based support such as CBT with exposure and response prevention where appropriate.
  • Keep shame low. OCD often grows when a young person feels too embarrassed to explain what is happening.

Bullying, social media and online safety

Bullying or online harm can drive anxiety, school avoidance, self-harm risk, sleep loss and shame.

Go deeper
  • Ask about friendship changes, group chats, image sharing, exclusion, threats, pressure, blackmail, humiliation or fear of school spaces.
  • Preserve evidence where safe, report through school/platform routes, and avoid blaming the young person for disclosure.
  • Review privacy, location, direct messages, comments and blocked accounts together.
  • If threats, coercion, sexual exploitation or blackmail are involved, treat it as safeguarding, not "drama".

Drugs, alcohol and risky coping

Substance use can be experimentation, social pressure or a coping strategy for anxiety, trauma, sleep or low mood.

Go deeper
  • Stay curious before punitive: what does it help them avoid, feel or belong to?
  • Be clear about safety boundaries and immediate risks, especially mixing substances, unknown pills or impaired consent.
  • Use specialist drug/alcohol advice if use is repeated, secretive, escalating or linked to mental health risk.
  • Connect it back to the wider support plan: sleep, peers, school, anxiety, mood and family communication.

ADHD, autism and SEND overlap

Unmet neurodevelopmental or learning needs can make school feel impossible and can be mistaken for behaviour or lack of effort.

Go deeper
  • Look for sensory overload, masking, shutdown after school, transitions, executive function difficulty, social exhaustion or rigid routines.
  • Gather examples across home and school before asking about support or assessment routes.
  • Speak to school SENCO/pastoral team about reasonable adjustments and support plans.
  • Use SEND Local Offer and reputable ADHD/autism resources alongside NHS guidance.

Trauma, bereavement and family stress

Loss, conflict, illness, abuse, family change, caring roles or frightening events can show up as anxiety, anger, shutdown or school absence.

Go deeper
  • Ask "what happened to you?" rather than "what is wrong with you?"
  • Keep routines predictable and give choices where possible.
  • Use safeguarding routes if abuse, neglect, exploitation or serious risk is suspected.
  • Consider bereavement, trauma-informed or family support alongside school planning.

School anxiety and avoidance

Plan around barriers, not blame

Many families and services use terms like emotionally based school avoidance because "refusal" can sound like a simple choice. The practical task is to identify barriers and make a return or attendance plan that is safe, specific and reviewable.

Barrier questions

  • When does distress start: night before, morning, journey, gate, first lesson, lunch, specific subject, end of day?
  • Is the pressure social, sensory, academic, relational, health-related, separation-based, trauma-linked or uncertainty-linked?
  • Is there bullying, friendship rupture, unmet SEND need, masking, perfectionism, fear of embarrassment, or fear of being trapped?
  • What helps even slightly: later start, quiet room, trusted adult, reduced timetable, pass card, home-school check-in, or predictable exit plan?

School meeting agenda

  1. Agree shared language: distress and barriers, not blame.
  2. Ask school what they observe before, during and after distress spikes.
  3. Choose one named trusted adult and one low-friction contact route.
  4. Agree the smallest attendance step that is genuinely achievable.
  5. Write down adjustments, owner, timescale and review date.
1

Map

List the high-stress points of the school day and rate each one.

2

Reduce one barrier at a time: arrival, lesson load, social exposure, sensory demand or uncertainty.

3

Use small rehearsable steps, not sudden full exposure after a long absence.

4

Meet again quickly. If a plan fails, treat it as data, not defiance.

Anxiety

Support anxiety without letting it run the system

Common signs

Avoidance, panic symptoms, reassurance seeking, irritability, tears, shutdown, sleep disruption, headaches, stomach pain, nausea, perfectionism or fear of being judged.

Helpful ingredients

Warm validation, predictable routines, problem solving, gradual exposure, good-enough sleep and food basics, school adjustments, and professional support when anxiety disrupts life.

Watch-outs

Endless reassurance can briefly soothe anxiety while strengthening it next time. Harsh pressure can also overwhelm. The middle path is compassionate structure.

Nausea and body symptoms

Take physical symptoms seriously

Anxiety can be physical, but physical symptoms deserve proper attention. Persistent, worsening or unexplained nausea should be discussed with a GP or appropriate clinician, especially if it affects eating, hydration, weight, school attendance or daily life.

Track before appointments

  • Time of day, duration, severity, vomiting, pain, bowel changes, dizziness, fever, weight change and hydration.
  • Sleep, food, caffeine, medication, menstrual cycle if relevant, school demands, social events and panic signs.
  • What helps: food, rest, leaving a situation, distraction, breathing, sleep, medication, routine or reassurance.

Ask a clinician

  • What physical causes should be ruled out?
  • What red flags should trigger urgent help?
  • Could symptoms fit reflux, constipation, migraine, medication effects, anxiety, functional nausea or another pattern?
  • What is the follow-up plan if symptoms continue?

SEND, ADHD and autism

Consider neurodevelopmental needs alongside mental health

Anxiety and school avoidance can be made worse by unmet sensory, attention, communication, social or learning needs. This does not mean every anxious teenager has ADHD or autism, but it is worth asking whether the environment is demanding skills that are currently overloaded.

Patterns to notice

Sensory overwhelm, masking then collapse, transition distress, executive function difficulty, social exhaustion, shutdown after school, rigid routines, intense uncertainty, or difficulty explaining what is wrong.

What to gather

Home and school examples, attendance patterns, sensory triggers, learning barriers, social communication concerns, sleep and regulation patterns, and what adjustments help.

Who to ask

School SENCO, pastoral team, GP, local SEND offer, Family Hub/Early Help, and relevant local ADHD or autism support routes.
