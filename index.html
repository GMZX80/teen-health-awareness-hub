Safety comes before strategy

This guide is for awareness, preparation and signposting. It is not medical advice, therapy, diagnosis, safeguarding advice, or a substitute for professional care.

If a young person is at immediate risk of harm, has taken an overdose, has a serious injury, cannot stay safe, or has urgent medical symptoms, seek emergency help immediately through local emergency services, A&E, NHS 111/999 as appropriate, or a crisis service.

Mental health crisis

Use NHS urgent mental health guidance, local crisis services, NHS 111, 999, or A&E depending on risk and urgency. Do not wait for a routine appointment if safety is at risk.

Self-harm concern

Stay calm, take the concern seriously, reduce access to means where possible, and seek professional support. Immediate danger is an emergency.

Working model

A practical way to hold the problem

Teen distress can be emotional, social, educational, physical and neurodevelopmental at the same time. A useful plan avoids blame, gathers patterns, and turns concern into small next actions.

1. Stabilise safety

Know what would make this urgent, who to contact, and what support is available outside normal hours. Make crisis information easy to find.

2. Understand the demand

School avoidance is often a response to demands that feel unmanageable: social pressure, sensory load, academic fear, transitions, uncertainty, separation, bullying or shame.

3. Reduce barriers

Progress usually comes from supported steps: predictable routines, one trusted adult, a safer arrival plan, adjusted expectations, and review dates.

4. Track patterns

Record sleep, food, nausea, panic signs, school events, social stress, sensory triggers, medication changes and recovery time. Patterns make professional conversations concrete.

5. Keep dignity central

Use language like distress, barriers, anxiety-related absence or emotionally based school avoidance. Avoid framing the young person as lazy, manipulative or defiant.

6. Review and adapt

Try one change at a time where possible. Keep what lowers distress or improves engagement. Stop what adds pressure without benefit.

Communication

Helpful parent and carer responses

What to say

  • "I can see this feels really hard. We are going to work out what is making it hard."
  • "You are not in trouble for feeling anxious. We still need a plan."
  • "Let's find the smallest step that feels possible, not perfect."
  • "Your body symptoms matter. We will track them and ask for medical advice if needed."

What to avoid

  • Arguing about whether the feeling is "real".
  • Using threats or shame to force attendance.
  • Assuming nausea is "just anxiety" without considering medical causes.
  • Making every plan in the middle of panic. Plan when everyone is calmer.

School anxiety and avoidance

Plan around barriers, not blame

Many families and services use terms like emotionally based school avoidance because "refusal" can sound like a simple choice. The practical task is to identify barriers and make a return or attendance plan that is safe, specific and reviewable.

Barrier questions

  • When does distress start: night before, morning, journey, gate, first lesson, lunch, specific subject, end of day?
  • Is the pressure social, sensory, academic, relational, health-related, separation-based, trauma-linked or uncertainty-linked?
  • Is there bullying, friendship rupture, unmet SEND need, masking, perfectionism, fear of embarrassment, or fear of being trapped?
  • What helps even slightly: later start, quiet room, trusted adult, reduced timetable, pass card, home-school check-in, or predictable exit plan?

School meeting agenda

  1. Agree shared language: distress and barriers, not blame.
  2. Ask school what they observe before, during and after distress spikes.
  3. Choose one named trusted adult and one low-friction contact route.
  4. Agree the smallest attendance step that is genuinely achievable.
  5. Write down adjustments, owner, timescale and review date.

Anxiety

Support anxiety without letting it run the system

Common signs

Avoidance, panic symptoms, reassurance seeking, irritability, tears, shutdown, sleep disruption, headaches, stomach pain, nausea, perfectionism or fear of being judged.

Helpful ingredients

Warm validation, predictable routines, problem solving, gradual exposure, good-enough sleep and food basics, school adjustments, and professional support when anxiety disrupts life.

Watch-outs

Endless reassurance can briefly soothe anxiety while strengthening it next time. Harsh pressure can also overwhelm. The middle path is compassionate structure.

Nausea and body symptoms

Take physical symptoms seriously

Anxiety can be physical, but physical symptoms deserve proper attention. Persistent, worsening or unexplained nausea should be discussed with a GP or appropriate clinician, especially if it affects eating, hydration, weight, school attendance or daily life.

Track before appointments

  • Time of day, duration, severity, vomiting, pain, bowel changes, dizziness, fever, weight change and hydration.
  • Sleep, food, caffeine, medication, menstrual cycle if relevant, school demands, social events and panic signs.
  • What helps: food, rest, leaving a situation, distraction, breathing, sleep, medication, routine or reassurance.

Ask a clinician

  • What physical causes should be ruled out?
  • What red flags should trigger urgent help?
  • Could symptoms fit reflux, constipation, migraine, medication effects, anxiety, functional nausea or another pattern?
  • What is the follow-up plan if symptoms continue?

SEND, ADHD and autism

Consider neurodevelopmental needs alongside mental health

Anxiety and school avoidance can be made worse by unmet sensory, attention, communication, social or learning needs. This does not mean every anxious teenager has ADHD or autism, but it is worth asking whether the environment is demanding skills that are currently overloaded.

Patterns to notice

Sensory overwhelm, masking then collapse, transition distress, executive function difficulty, social exhaustion, shutdown after school, rigid routines, intense uncertainty, or difficulty explaining what is wrong.

What to gather

Home and school examples, attendance patterns, sensory triggers, learning barriers, social communication concerns, sleep and regulation patterns, and what adjustments help.

Who to ask

School SENCO, pastoral team, GP, local SEND offer, Family Hub/Early Help, and relevant local ADHD or autism support routes.
