How to use this

The useful question is usually "who owns this?"

Acronyms are not the real problem. The problem is when nobody is clear about the decision, the owner, the timescale, or the contact route. In meetings, translate every acronym into: what it means, why it matters, who is responsible, and what happens next.

Acronym decoder

Common terms in plain English

Terms vary by area. If a local service uses a slightly different meaning, ask them to write it down in the meeting notes.

CAMHS / CYPS

Usually means: Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services, or Children and Young People's Services.

Plain English: Specialist NHS mental-health support for children and teenagers where need, impairment or risk is beyond ordinary school or early-help support.

Ask: "Is this advice, assessment, treatment, crisis support, or a referral decision?"

MHST

Usually means: Mental Health Support Team.

Plain English: School-linked early mental-health support, often for mild to moderate anxiety, low mood or emotional wellbeing needs.

Ask: "Is our school covered, and what can they offer while we wait?"

PMHW

Usually means: Primary Mental Health Worker.

Plain English: A professional who may advise, assess, offer brief support, consult with school, or help decide whether specialist referral is needed.

Ask: "Are they working directly with the young person, advising school, or helping with triage?"

SEND / SEN

Usually means: Special Educational Needs and Disabilities, or Special Educational Needs.

Plain English: Learning, communication, sensory, attention, physical, emotional or developmental needs that affect access to education.

Ask: "What barriers has school identified, and what support is being tried?"

SENCO

Usually means: Special Educational Needs Coordinator.

Plain English: The school lead for SEND support, reasonable adjustments, evidence gathering and SEN support review.

Ask: "Can we agree the support plan, owner, review date and evidence needed?"

EHCP / EHCNA

Usually means: Education, Health and Care Plan / Education, Health and Care Needs Assessment.

Plain English: A statutory SEND assessment and, if agreed, a legal plan for special educational provision.

Ask: "Are ordinary SEN support steps enough, or should we request an EHC needs assessment?"

EBSA

Usually means: Emotionally Based School Avoidance.

Plain English: School non-attendance or difficulty attending because distress, anxiety, sensory load, social pressure, health symptoms or other barriers feel unmanageable.

Ask: "Which part of the school day is the barrier, and what adjustment are we testing first?"

TAF

Usually means: Team Around the Family.

Plain English: A multi-agency meeting or plan where family, school and services agree actions together.

Ask: "Who is the lead professional, and when will the plan be reviewed?"

Early Help / EHA

Usually means: Early Help, sometimes Early Help Assessment.

Plain English: Coordinated support before problems become severe enough for statutory social care, often involving family support and school.

Ask: "What is the goal of Early Help here, and what would trigger stepping up?"

MARF / MASH

Usually means: Multi-Agency Referral Form / Multi-Agency Safeguarding Hub.

Plain English: Referral or front-door route for safeguarding or children's social-care concerns. Names vary locally.

Ask: "Is this being raised as early help, child in need, or safeguarding?"

CIN / CP

Usually means: Child in Need / Child Protection.

Plain English: Statutory social-care categories. Child in Need is support under section 17. Child Protection is used where significant harm is suspected or likely.

Ask: "What threshold has been met, what is the plan, and what needs to change?"

MDT

Usually means: Multi-Disciplinary Team.

Plain English: Professionals from different disciplines discussing a case, such as health, education, therapy or social care.

Ask: "Who attended, what was decided, and who is telling the family?"

Role map

Who is usually responsible for what?

Job titles can sound authoritative without making the next step clearer. This map helps separate clinical decisions, education support, safeguarding, coordination and daily implementation.

Parent or carer

Usually owns: Consent where required, home observations, appointments, safety at home, sharing patterns, and saying when a plan is not workable.

Does not own alone: Coordinating every agency, diagnosing, designing school provision, or holding serious risk without professional support.

School pastoral lead

Usually owns: Day-to-day wellbeing support, attendance planning, trusted adult arrangements, communication with family, and school-based safeguarding actions.

Ask for: A named contact, agreed response times, and written actions after meetings.

SENCO

Usually owns: SEN support, reasonable adjustments, evidence of barriers, review cycles, and liaison around SEND processes.

Ask for: What has been tried, what changed, what evidence exists, and whether further assessment is needed.

GP or primary care clinician

Usually owns: Physical health review, medication questions, initial clinical advice, referrals where appropriate, and red-flag guidance.

Ask for: What physical causes need ruling out and what to do if symptoms worsen.

School nurse or public health nurse

Usually owns: Health advice, early support, signposting and sometimes targeted work around sleep, wellbeing, self-esteem, low-level self-harm or emotional health.

Ask for: Whether they can support directly, advise school, or refer onward.

Early Help or family support

Usually owns: Coordinating family-focused support when needs cross home, school and community but do not necessarily meet social-care thresholds.

Ask for: A lead professional, goals, actions, review date and step-up criteria.

CYPS / CAMHS clinician

Usually owns: Specialist mental-health assessment, formulation, treatment planning, risk planning and clinical recommendations within their service remit.

Ask for: The outcome, diagnosis if any, formulation, treatment plan, waiting-time advice, and who to contact in crisis.

Children's social care

Usually owns: Statutory assessment where thresholds are met, child-in-need planning, child protection processes, and safeguarding decisions.

Ask for: The threshold decision, allocated worker if any, plan status, and what would change the level of involvement.

Meeting language

Questions that cut through confusion

These questions are deliberately simple. They work because they turn professional shorthand into a concrete plan.

Meaning

  • "What does that acronym mean in plain English?"
  • "Is that a service, a process, a legal threshold, or a person?"
  • "Does that term mean the same thing locally as it does nationally?"

Responsibility

  • "Who owns this decision?"
  • "Who is doing the next action, and by when?"
  • "Who should we contact if symptoms, attendance or risk changes?"

Escalation

  • "Is this urgent, routine, or just for information?"
  • "What would make this a crisis or safeguarding concern?"
  • "What happens if this plan does not work?"

Evidence

  • "What information do you need from us?"
  • "What evidence does school or the service already have?"
  • "Can we record the agreed wording in the notes?"
