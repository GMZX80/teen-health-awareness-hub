Important

This is an orientation map, not a referral decision tool

Local pathways change and individual risk matters. Use this page to prepare conversations with school, GP, Early Help, CYPS/CAMHS or social care. If a young person cannot stay safe, has taken an overdose, is seriously injured, is missing, or there is immediate danger, use emergency or crisis routes rather than waiting for the normal pathway.

Fast overview

The support ladder

Most systems are built around graduated support: start with universal help if it is enough, add targeted help when needs persist, and step up to specialist or statutory services when complexity, impairment or risk increases.

1

Universal support

Home, school pastoral support, SENCO, GP, school nursing, trusted adult, attendance plan, ordinary reasonable adjustments.

2

Targeted early help

Family Hub/Early Help, Team Around the Family, school nurse targeted input, Mental Health Support Team where available, Primary Mental Health Worker.

3

Specialist health or statutory help

CYPS/CAMHS assessment and treatment, specialist eating disorder or neurodevelopmental routes, educational psychology, EHC needs assessment, Child in Need support.

4

Risk, protection or placement

Crisis response, hospital assessment/admission, section 47 child protection, looked-after child planning, secure welfare accommodation only where strict legal criteria are met.

Detailed pathway

Levels of help and the usual steps

Northumberland's children's services threshold model describes four broad levels of need. The Be You Northumberland mental-health response uses similar language: coping, getting help, getting more help and getting risk support.

Level What it can look like Who is usually involved What normally needs to happen Examples of help When to step up
Level 1
Universal / coping		 Mild anxiety, occasional absence, friendship stress, sleep disruption, early nausea linked to stress, young person still mostly functioning. Parent/carer, young person, school tutor or pastoral lead, SENCO if SEND may be relevant, GP for physical symptoms, school nurse. Talk with the young person, ask school what they see, check health red flags, agree simple adjustments, record patterns for a short period. Named trusted adult, check-in/check-out, quiet arrival, reduced uncertainty, homework adjustment, school nurse advice, GP review for nausea or sleep. Distress lasts several weeks, attendance drops, symptoms worsen, basic routines are affected, or the plan is not enough.
Level 2
Additional needs / early help / getting help		 Persistent school avoidance, escalating anxiety, family strain, mild to moderate self-harm concern, low mood, emerging SEND questions, multiple services starting to be relevant. School pastoral/SENCO, Early Help First Contact, Family Hub, school nurse, Mental Health Support Team where available, Primary Mental Health Worker, GP. Seek consent, gather evidence of what has already been tried, complete Early Help or service referral routes, hold a Team Around the Family-style plan with owners and review dates. Early Help support request, Team Around the Family action plan, time-limited goal-based mental-health input, parent support, attendance support plan, targeted school adjustments. Moderate to severe distress, first-line help has not worked, risk is identified, functioning is significantly impaired, or specialist assessment may be needed.
Level 3
Specialist / statutory / getting more help		 Significant psychological distress, complex anxiety/depression, eating disorder concern, repeated self-harm, severe impairment, complex SEND, disability, or need for statutory social-care assessment. CYPS/CAMHS, GP, school, Primary Mental Health Worker, educational psychologist, local authority SEND team, children's social care under Child in Need where threshold is met. Make a detailed referral with consent, previous interventions and outcomes, risk plan, school information and physical health information. Consider EHC needs assessment if education cannot meet needs from ordinary support. CYPS assessment and treatment, CBT or family work where appropriate, medication review where clinically indicated, specialist eating-disorder service, educational psychology advice, EHC needs assessment, Child in Need plan. Risk becomes acute, there is suspected significant harm, the young person cannot stay safe, hospital-level care may be needed, or home/school placement is breaking down.
Level 4
Protection / crisis / getting risk support		 Immediate safety risk, overdose, serious self-harm, suicidal plan or intent, exploitation, abuse or neglect concern, repeated missing episodes, serious family breakdown, significant harm threshold. NHS 111 mental-health option, 999/A&E where emergency, CYPS crisis/intensive services, police if immediate danger or missing, children's social care, safeguarding leads, hospital teams. Do not wait for routine meetings. Contact urgent/crisis or safeguarding routes. Children's social care decides whether child protection or other statutory action is required. Crisis assessment, home treatment where appropriate, emergency medical care, section 47 enquiry, child protection plan, looked-after child planning, specialist inpatient care if clinically required. If restrictions on liberty or secure welfare accommodation are being considered, this is a legal/statutory placement decision, not an ordinary mental-health referral step.
Exceptional edge
Secure welfare accommodation		 Very high risk where a child is likely to run away and suffer significant harm, or likely to injure themselves or someone else in any other accommodation. Local authority children's social care, legal services, court, Department for Education approval routes for under-13s, secure children's home, education and health teams. The local authority must evidence the legal criteria, alternatives tried or considered, care plan, therapeutic needs, intended outcomes and exit strategy. Secure children's home placement with care, education and health provision. This is usually for children with complex histories and serious risk, not a routine CAMHS destination. Review must focus on safety, therapeutic purpose, legal authority, least restrictive option and return/transition planning.

Who does what

How the services interact

The same teenager may need support from several places at once. The key is to make each agency's role explicit so the family is not left coordinating everything alone.

Service Main role Typical trigger What they need from others What families can ask for
School Daily observation, pastoral care, attendance planning, safeguarding, SEN support, adjustments, communication with family. Attendance drops, distress in school, bullying, anxiety around lessons, suspected SEND, safety concern disclosed in school. Parent/carer pattern notes, GP/CYPS letters where relevant, young person's views, safeguarding information, clear consent where needed. Named trusted adult, SENCO meeting, attendance support plan, reasonable adjustments, risk assessment, safe space, reduced timetable with review, referral support.
GP / primary care Physical health assessment, medication review, first clinical advice, referral/signposting to mental-health or paediatric services where appropriate. Nausea, vomiting, pain, sleep, weight change, panic symptoms, low mood, self-harm concern, family wants medical advice. Symptom timeline, school attendance pattern, risk information, current support, medications, what has changed and how quickly. What red flags to watch for, what physical causes should be ruled out, whether referral is needed, what to do while waiting.
School nursing / Public Health School Nursing Health promotion, early identification, advice and targeted support for school-age children, including emotional wellbeing and lower-level health concerns. Low to moderate anxiety, sleep, self-esteem, low-level self-harm, relationship difficulty, health issue affecting school. Consent, school context, family concerns, previous preliminary work and what impact remains. Advice session, targeted support, referral onward to Primary Mental Health or CYPS if needed.
Family Hub / Early Help Coordinate multi-agency support where needs are more than one service can solve alone but do not necessarily meet social-care threshold. Family stress, school avoidance, unclear needs, parent support need, multiple early concerns, no existing Early Help Assessment. Consent, school information, family strengths/worries, what help has been tried, which professionals are already involved. Early Help support request, Team Around the Family plan, parenting support, signposting, review meetings, step-up if risk increases.
MHST / Primary Mental Health Worker Focused, goal-based early mental-health intervention and advice, often for mild to moderate or moderate needs that have not improved with first-line help. Anxiety, low mood, emotional regulation or school distress affecting functioning, but not clearly requiring specialist CYPS at the outset. Previous first-line interventions and outcomes, young person's goals, school context, risk information, consent. Assessment, brief intervention, parent/carer advice, school consultation, signposting or onward referral.
CYPS / CAMHS Specialist assessment and treatment for significant mental-health difficulty, including more complex cases and some intensive community support. Moderate to severe distress, functional impairment, risk, complex presentation, previous early help has not been enough. Consent, detailed referral, evidence of previous help and outcomes, risk plan, school/GP information, physical health context. Referral criteria check, consultation slot, assessment outcome, treatment plan, crisis plan, who to contact while waiting.
SEND / Educational Psychology Understand learning, communication, social, sensory and developmental barriers; advise schools; contribute to EHC needs assessments. School cannot meet need from ordinary support, suspected SEND, persistent anxiety linked to learning/sensory/social demands, repeated failed attendance plans. School evidence, parent and young person's views, attainment and attendance data, examples of adjustments tried and impact. SEN support review, educational psychology input, EHC needs assessment request, clear outcomes and provision.
Children's social care Assess child-in-need, safeguarding, child protection, family support, looked-after child or placement needs where statutory thresholds are met. Suspected abuse/neglect/exploitation, significant harm risk, family breakdown, missing episodes, disability with unmet statutory need, high risk that cannot be held by universal services. Clear worry, harm/risk evidence, immediate safety information, family strengths, existing plans, professional views. Threshold decision, Child and Family Assessment, Child in Need plan, child protection response, step-down to Early Help when risk reduces.

Good referrals

What makes a referral or meeting useful

Describe functioning

Attendance, sleep, eating, self-care, friendships, lessons, family life, panic, nausea, avoidance, recovery time and what has changed.

Show what has been tried

Adjustments, pastoral work, trusted adult, GP review, school nurse, Early Help, parent strategies, brief interventions and outcomes.

Be explicit about risk

Self-harm, suicidal thoughts, running away, exploitation, abuse, aggression, overdose, not eating/drinking, or inability to stay safe.

Urgent routes

When the normal ladder is too slow

Use urgent help if safety or health cannot wait. In North East and North Cumbria, NHS 111 mental-health option is available for all ages, including children and young people.

Immediate danger

Call 999 or go to A&E if there is immediate risk to life, serious injury, overdose, severe medical symptoms, or the young person cannot be kept safe.

Urgent mental-health help

Call NHS 111 and select the mental-health option. The service can advise, listen and connect to crisis services where needed.

NHS 111 mental-health option

Northumberland CYPS

CYPS accepts professional referrals and self-referrals in line with criteria. For urgent or emergency referrals requiring response within 24 hours, current CNTW guidance says to call NHS 111 and select the mental-health option.
